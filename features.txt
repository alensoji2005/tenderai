1. Optimal "Price-to-Win" (P2W) Recommender
Right now, the ML model predicts the probability of winning based on a given price. You could invert this to create a Price Optimization Engine.

How it works: Instead of the user inputting a price to get a probability, the user inputs their base cost. The engine runs thousands of simulations to recommend the exact bid price that maximizes profit margins while keeping the win probability above a safe threshold (e.g., 85%).
Tech: Monte Carlo simulations layered on top of your existing TensorFlow/PyTorch model, or a reinforcement learning approach.
2. RFP / Tender Risk Extraction (NLP/LLMs)
Tender documents often contain hundreds of pages with buried penalties, unusual technical clauses, or strict delivery requirements.

How it works: Integrate an LLM (like GPT-4, Claude, or a local Llama 3 model) to automatically "read" the scraped PDFs. It would highlight dealbreaker clauses, calculate liquidated damages risks, and summarize the core requirements into a 1-page brief for management.
Tech: LangChain/LlamaIndex, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) using the scraped PDFs.
3. Competitor Behavior Prediction
Go beyond just analyzing past competitor behavior by actively predicting their next moves.

How it works: When a new tender drops, the ML model predicts which specific competitors are likely to bid on it and estimates what their bid prices will be, based on their recent win/loss streaks, current estimated backlog (are they busy?), and historical aggressiveness.
Tech: Multi-class classification for competitor participation, and regression models for competitor price estimation.
4. Automated "Bid / No-Bid" Decision Matrix
Bidding on tenders is expensive in terms of human hours.

How it works: An AI-driven scoring system that instantly evaluates a newly scraped tender and recommends whether it's even worth the company's time to bid. It would score based on historical win rates in that specific category, current factory capacity, and alignment with company strengths.
5. Raw Material Cost Forecasting
Electrical equipment pricing (transformers, cables, switchgears) is highly dependent on raw material costs like copper, aluminum, and steel.

How it works: Integrate live commodities market data APIs. The engine predicts what the material costs will be at the time of manufacturing/delivery (which could be 6-12 months after the bid is submitted), preventing the company from winning a bid but losing money due to inflation.
Tech: Time-series forecasting algorithms (ARIMA, Prophet, or LSTM networks).
6. BOQ (Bill of Quantities) Anomaly Detection
How it works: When your scraper parses the BOQ from a new tender, an ML model flags items that are unusually priced or quantities that seem mathematically incorrect based on similar past projects. This helps catch government typos or allows your team to strategically price certain line items (unbalanced bidding) where you know the government estimates are off.
